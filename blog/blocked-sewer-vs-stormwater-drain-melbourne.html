Two Separate Systems, Two Different Jobs

Almost every Melbourne home has two completely separate underground drainage networks, and mixing them up is the single most common reason people call the wrong person or clear the wrong pipe. Your sewer takes dirty wastewater — everything from your toilets, showers, basins, bath, kitchen and laundry — and carries it to the water authority's main for treatment. Your stormwater takes clean rainwater off your roof, downpipes, pits and paved areas and drains it away to a legal discharge point, usually a street kerb, a council drain or a creek.

They never connect (it's illegal to plumb one into the other), they run in different pipes, and they block for different reasons. So the fastest way to work out which one is in trouble is to ask a simple question: is the trouble coming from inside the house, or from the rain outside? That one distinction sorts the vast majority of cases before anyone lifts a grate.

Signs It's Your Sewer

A blocked sewer shows up at your fixtures — the things you use every day inside. The tell-tale signs are:

🔊 Gurgling across fixtures

Flush the toilet and the shower drain gurgles, or empty the sink and the toilet bubbles. When more than one fixture reacts, the blockage is in the shared main line, not a single trap.

👃 Sewage smell

A rotten-egg or raw-sewage smell inside the house or drifting up from a gully outside means wastewater is sitting where it shouldn't and gases are escaping back up the line.

🐌 Everything draining slow

One slow sink is a local clog. Every drain in the house running slow at the same time points to the main sewer line downstream of all of them.

🚽 Toilet levels moving

The bowl rising when you run a tap, or dropping low after a flush, is air being pushed and pulled past a partial blockage in the sewer.

⚠️ Overflow relief gully spilling

That short capped or grated pipe outside, lower than your gutters, is designed to spill sewage outside before it comes up inside. If it's overflowing, the sewer is blocked — act fast.

🌳 It keeps coming back

In Melbourne's leafy east, recurring sewer blockages are almost always tree roots feeding on the pipe — a temporary snake won't hold.

The number-one cause of blocked sewers in the Ranges and Yarra Valley is tree roots working into old terracotta joints, followed by grease, wet wipes and foreign objects. If you're getting the signs above, it's a job for a blocked sewer plumber — and because sewage is a health risk, it's the more urgent of the two.

Signs It's Your Stormwater

A blocked stormwater drain shows up when it rains — and often only when it rains, which is why it catches people out. Watch for:

🌧️ Downpipes overflowing

Water backing up and spilling out the top of a downpipe, or gutters overflowing in a downpour, means the stormwater line below can't take it away fast enough.

🕳️ Pits and grates backing up

A stormwater pit that fills and holds water long after the rain, or a grate that bubbles up instead of draining down, is a clear sign the pipe past it is choked.

💧 Yard or garage pooling

Water sitting in the yard, flooding into the garage, or running across the driveway during rain points to a stormwater line that's blocked or collapsed downstream.

🏠 Moisture near foundations

Damp getting into the base of walls or pooling against the slab after every storm is stormwater with nowhere to go — a slow risk to the building, not just a nuisance.

Stormwater blocks up from leaves and garden debris jamming pits, silt and soil building up over years, tree roots in the joints, and cracked or collapsed old terracotta. The good news: no sewage, so it's less hazardous. The catch: left alone it can undermine foundations and flood a garage, so it's still worth clearing properly. That's what our stormwater drain service is for, and a stormwater health check before storm season catches it early.

Who's Responsible — and Who to Call

This is where a lot of Melburnians get stuck, because responsibility splits differently for each system. The simple version: anything on your property, up to the connection point, is yours — so a plumber is your first call for both a blocked sewer and a blocked stormwater drain.

Beyond your boundary they part ways. The sewer main in the street belongs to your water authority — across Melbourne's east that's Yarra Valley Water — and if the blockage is on their side, they'll clear it free and can even reimburse a plumber's call-out you paid to investigate. Public stormwater drains under roads, footpaths and parks belong to your local council, while the big regional drains, creeks and flood channels are Melbourne Water's. If water's pooling on your block or your downpipes are backing up, that's almost always on your side of the line. How We Confirm It — CCTV on Every Job

Symptoms narrow it down, but the pipe gives the final answer. On any main-line job I run a CCTV drain camera down the line so you can see on screen exactly which system is blocked, where the blockage sits, and what's causing it — roots, grease, silt or a collapsed section. That means no clearing the wrong pipe and no guessing before you spend money.

Once we've confirmed it, the fix follows the cause. A root-choked sewer or a silted stormwater line usually gets high-pressure hydro jetting — a 5000 PSI jet that scours the full pipe wall clean, not just a hole through the middle like a basic snake. If the camera shows a cracked or collapsed pipe underneath, pipe relining repairs it from the inside without digging up your yard. Multiple fixtures affected at once points to the main sewer line, which usually means tree roots in Melbourne's east.

Blocked stormwater shows up in the rain: downpipes overflowing at the top, gutters spilling, stormwater pits and grates filling up and backing out, water pooling in the yard, garage or driveway, and moisture or water sitting near the foundations after a downpour. Stormwater problems are usually caused by leaves, silt, garden debris, tree roots or a collapsed section of old terracotta pipe.

They can, especially in older homes in the Dandenong Ranges and Yarra Valley where both lines are aged terracotta and share the same tree roots nearby. Because the symptoms can overlap — both can gurgle, smell or pool — the only reliable way to separate them is a CCTV camera down each line.
